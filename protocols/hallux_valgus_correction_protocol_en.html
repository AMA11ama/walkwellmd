Hallux Valgus Correction — Rehabilitation Protocol

WalkWellMD · Dr. Ali Alhaddad Clinic instruction sheet

Urgent concerns? If you develop fever, severe pain/swelling, calf pain, shortness of breath, wound discharge, or numb/blue toes: Book | 0127444444 or go to the nearest Emergency Department.

Weight-bearing: Heel WBAT immediately in a post-operative shoe. Avoid pressure on the forefoot/big toe.

Follow-up Visits

  • Day 3: wound check
  • Day 9: wound check and X-rays
  • Day 15: suture removal
  • Then every 2 weeks until full bone healing is confirmed

Early Care (Weeks 0–2)

  • Immobilization: Bulky dressing or post-operative shoe. Keep clean/dry; do not remove until the surgeon confirms wound healing.
  • Elevation: Foot above heart level as much as possible.
  • Weight-bearing: Walk on your heel only using your post-op shoe; use crutches for support if needed.
  • Medication:
    • Antibiotics for 7 days — complete the full course.
    • Pain control:
      • First 72 hours: take prescribed narcotic + regular non-narcotic regularly.
      • Days 4–6: take the non-narcotic regularly.
      • From day 7: take pain medication as needed only.
  • Blood clot prevention: Aspirin once daily for 14 days unless instructed otherwise.
  • Precautions: No driving, no sports, no barefoot walking, and no pressure on the forefoot.

Weeks 2–6

  • Continue heel-only walking using the post-op shoe.
  • Sutures removed around day 15. Dressing changes in clinic only.
  • Begin gentle ankle/toe motion (exclude the operated big toe).
  • Continue elevation and ice as needed for swelling.

Weeks 6–8

  • X-rays to assess bone healing.
  • If healing is satisfactory: gradually transition to full weight-bearing in a stiff-soled or rocker-bottom shoe.
  • Begin gentle gait training with physiotherapy.
  • Avoid running, jumping, or excessive big-toe bending.

Weeks 8–12

  • Continue physiotherapy for ankle and forefoot flexibility (avoid stressing the surgical site).
  • Focus on balance, strength, and normal gait.
  • Return to desk work and most daily activities.
  • Avoid high heels or tight shoes.

Months 3–6

  • Gradual return to full activity as comfort allows.
  • Most patients resume normal walking and light sports (cycling, swimming) by 3–4 months.
  • Full recovery typically by 4–6 months.

Swelling & Elevation

  • Swelling is normal and may last several months.
  • Keep foot elevated above heart level when resting.
  • Ice packs 15–20 minutes several times daily, especially after activity.
  • Swelling often increases at day’s end — rest and elevate.
