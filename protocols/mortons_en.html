Morton’s Neuroma Excision – Rehabilitation Protocol

By WalkWellMD (clinic instruction sheet)

Follow-up Visits

  • Day 3: wound check
  • Day 9: wound check
  • Day 15: suture removal
  • Then every 2 weeks until fully healed

Early Care (Weeks 0–2)

  • Immobilization: Bandage or post-operative shoe. Do not remove. Keep dry until your surgeon confirms full wound healing and gives permission.
  • Elevation: Keep the foot above heart level as much as possible to reduce swelling.
  • Weight-bearing: Walk carefully in the post-op shoe; avoid full pressure on the ball of the foot.
  • Medication:
    • Antibiotics for 7 days — complete the course.
    • Pain management:
      • First 72 hours: take both the stronger (narcotic) and standard (non-narcotic) pain medicines regularly regardless of pain.
      • Days 4–6: take the non-narcotic regularly regardless of pain.
      • From day 7: take pain medication only if needed.
  • Blood clot prevention: Aspirin once daily for 14 days unless instructed otherwise.
  • Precautions: No driving, sports, barefoot walking, or heavy lifting.

Weeks 2–4

  • Continue using the post-op shoe until cleared by your surgeon.
  • Short walks are allowed; avoid high-impact activity.
  • Sutures removed around day 15.
  • Begin gentle toe motion after suture removal.

Weeks 4–6

  • Transition gradually to supportive shoes with a wide toe box.
  • Begin physiotherapy:
    • Scar massage once the wound is fully healed.
    • Gentle toe stretching.
    • Gait training to correct limping.
  • Still avoid running, jumping, or narrow shoes.

Weeks 6–10

  • Physiotherapy focus:
    • Foot-muscle strengthening.
    • Balance and proprioception exercises.
  • Increase walking distances as tolerated; pain should be minimal.

Months 3+

  • Gradual return to all daily activities.
  • Return to sport once cleared by your surgeon (typically ~3–4 months).
  • Long-term: avoid tight/narrow shoes and high heels to reduce recurrence risk.

Red Flags — Call Us Immediately

  • Fever > 38°C
  • Worsening pain or swelling not relieved by rest/medication
  • Wound redness, pus, or foul odor
  • Calf pain, swelling, or sudden shortness of breath
  • Numbness or spreading tingling in the toes

Swelling & Elevation

  • Swelling is normal after surgery and may last for several months.
  • Keep the foot elevated above heart level when resting.
  • Use ice packs 15–20 minutes at a time, several times daily, especially after activity.
  • Swelling may increase at day’s end — rest and elevate to reduce it.
