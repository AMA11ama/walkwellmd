Open Reduction & Internal Fixation (ORIF) — Fifth Metatarsal Base Fracture

Rigid plate-and-screw fixation · Patient instructions (WalkWellMD)

Follow-up Visits

  • Day 3: wound check
  • Day 9: wound check and X-rays
  • Day 15: suture removal
  • Then every 2 weeks until radiographic union

Early Care (Weeks 0–2)

  • Immobilization: below-knee back slab or cast. Keep dry; do not remove.
  • Elevation above heart level as much as possible.
  • Non-weight bearing on the operated foot; use crutches or a walker.
  • Medication:
    • Antibiotics: 7 days — complete the full course.
    • Pain control: stronger+nonnarcotic regularly for first 72 h; days 4–6 non-narcotic regularly; from day 7 as needed.
  • Blood-clot prevention: Aspirin daily ×14 days unless instructed otherwise.
  • Precautions: no driving, no sports, no barefoot walking; keep cast dry and protected.

Weeks 2–4

  • Replace cast/back slab with an Air Cast / CAM boot.
  • Begin heel weight-bearing only as tolerated; continue crutches.
  • Keep all weight off the forefoot.
  • Wound should be clean and dry.
  • Gentle ankle ROM (within boot) may start if approved.
  • Continue elevation and ice for swelling.

Weeks 4–6

  • X-rays to assess bone healing.
  • Once cleared, progress to full weight-bearing in the boot.
  • Physiotherapy:
    • Ankle mobility and peroneal strengthening
    • Balance and proprioception
    • Gait training in boot
  • Avoid running, jumping, or impact activities.

Weeks 6–8

  • Continue full weight-bearing in boot until permitted to transition to supportive shoes.
  • Remove boot once radiographic union confirmed.
  • Progress physiotherapy to strength, balance, and gait correction.

Weeks 8–12

  • Walk in supportive shoes with a firm sole.
  • Continue physiotherapy for endurance and agility.
  • Return to desk work and most light daily activities.

Months 3–6

  • Gradual return to sport-specific training after confirmed bone union.
  • Low-impact exercise (cycling, swimming) → jogging ≈ 4 months → cutting/pivoting sports ≈ 5–6 months.

Red Flags — Call Us Immediately

  • Fever > 38 °C
  • Worsening pain or swelling not relieved by rest/medication
  • Wound redness, pus, or foul odor
  • Calf pain/swelling, or sudden shortness of breath
  • Numbness or color change of toes
Action: contact our clinic at 0127444444 to schedule an appointment as soon as possible, or go to the emergency department.

Swelling & Elevation

  • Swelling is normal for several months.
  • Keep foot elevated when resting.
  • Use ice 15–20 min several times daily, especially after activity.
  • Swelling may increase later in the day — rest and elevate to reduce it.
